<h2 id="what-is-dca">What Is Dollar-Cost Averaging</h2>

<p>Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is an investing strategy where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, such as $200 every month, regardless of whether the market is up or down. The strategy removes the pressure of deciding when to buy because you buy on a preset schedule.</p>

<p>The mathematical benefit is straightforward: when prices drop, your fixed dollar amount buys more shares, and when prices rise, it buys fewer shares. Over time, this tends to lower your average cost per share compared to making sporadic purchases based on market sentiment.</p>

<p>Most people already use dollar-cost averaging without realizing it. If you contribute to a <a href="/blog/what-is-401k-employer-matching">401(k)</a> through paycheck deductions, you are dollar-cost averaging. The same applies to any automatic investment plan in a <a href="/blog/how-to-open-roth-ira-2026">Roth IRA</a> or brokerage account. The strategy works because it turns investing into a habit rather than a decision.</p>

<h2 id="dca-vs-lump-sum">DCA vs. Lump-Sum Investing</h2>

<p>If you have a large sum of money, should you invest it all at once or spread it out? A landmark 2025 Vanguard study analyzed 92 years of data across the U.S., U.K., and Australian markets. The finding: investing a lump sum immediately outperformed dollar-cost averaging 68% of the time.</p>

<p>The reason is simple. Markets go up more often than they go down. If you spread your investment over 12 months, you keep money on the sideline during months when the market is likely rising. Over the long term, this costs you returns compared to being fully invested from day one.</p>

<p>However, DCA wins on one dimension that numbers cannot capture: emotional comfort. A 2025 Schwab study found that investors who use DCA are 73% less likely to panic-sell during a market downturn. If lump-sum investing makes you lose sleep, DCA is the better strategy for you because the worst investment plan is the one you abandon. As <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dollarcostaveraging.asp" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Investopedia</a> notes, the behavioral advantage of DCA often outweighs its mathematical disadvantage.</p>

<h2 id="dca-in-practice">How DCA Works in Practice</h2>

<p>Let us say you invest $500 per month in an S&P 500 index fund. In January, the fund price is $50, so you buy 10 shares. In February, the price drops to $40, so your $500 buys 12.5 shares. In March, the price rebounds to $55, so you buy 9.1 shares. After three months, you own 31.6 shares at an average cost of $47.47 per share.</p>

<p>If you had invested the full $1,500 in January at $50, you would own only 30 shares. By dollar-cost averaging, you accumulated 1.6 more shares because you bought heavily during the February dip. This effect becomes more pronounced over years of consistent investing through multiple market cycles.</p>

<p>The power of DCA compounds over time alongside <a href="/blog/compound-interest-explained">compound interest</a>. Those extra shares you accumulate during market dips earn their own returns, which buy more shares, which earn more returns. This cycle is exactly how ordinary investors build six-figure and seven-figure portfolios over 20 to 30 years.</p>

<h2 id="setting-up-auto">Setting Up Automatic Investments</h2>

<p>Every major brokerage makes automatic investing simple. At Fidelity, navigate to "Automatic Investments" in your account settings, select your fund (such as VTI or FZROX), choose your contribution amount, and set your schedule. The process takes less than five minutes.</p>

<p>The best schedule for DCA is the one that matches your paycheck. If you are paid biweekly, set up biweekly investments. If monthly, invest monthly. The specific day of the month does not matter statistically. A 2025 analysis by <a href="https://www.bankrate.com/investing/dollar-cost-averaging/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Bankrate</a> found no meaningful return difference between investing on the 1st, 15th, or any other day of the month.</p>

<p>Start with an amount you can sustain without stress. It is better to invest $100 per month consistently for 10 years than to invest $500 per month for 6 months and then stop. Read our guide on <a href="/blog/how-much-to-invest-per-month-beginner">how much beginners should invest per month</a> for specific budgeting frameworks.</p>

<h2 id="when-dca-makes-sense">When DCA Makes the Most Sense</h2>

<p><strong>Regular income:</strong> If your investable money comes from a paycheck rather than a windfall, DCA is the natural and optimal approach. You invest each paycheck's surplus as it arrives. There is no lump sum to debate about, so DCA is the default strategy for most working investors.</p>

<p><strong>Volatile markets:</strong> During periods of high uncertainty, DCA protects you from investing everything right before a crash. If you are worried about a potential <a href="/blog/how-to-invest-during-recession">recession</a>, spreading your investment over 6 to 12 months reduces your timing risk significantly.</p>

<p><strong>Emotional investors:</strong> If you find yourself checking stock prices daily or losing sleep over market swings, DCA automates the process and takes emotion out of the equation. The best investors are often the most boring ones. Set your automatic investment, delete your brokerage app from your home screen, and check your portfolio quarterly at most.</p>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

<h3>Does dollar-cost averaging work with individual stocks?</h3>

<p>DCA works with any investment, but it is most effective with diversified funds like S&P 500 ETFs. Individual stocks can go to zero, and DCA into a declining stock just means buying more of a losing position. With index funds, temporary declines almost always recover because the broad market trends upward over time.</p>

<h3>How long should I dollar-cost average?</h3>

<p>For regular income-based investing, DCA should be your permanent strategy for as long as you are earning money. For a lump sum you want to deploy gradually, most advisors recommend spreading it over 6 to 12 months. Going beyond 12 months leaves too much money uninvested for too long and increases the chance of underperforming a lump-sum approach.</p>

<h3>Can I dollar-cost average into a Roth IRA?</h3>

<p>Absolutely. You can set up automatic monthly contributions to your <a href="/blog/roth-ira-vs-traditional-ira">Roth IRA</a> at any brokerage. For example, contributing $583.33 per month reaches the $7,000 annual limit by December. Many investors round to $600 per month for 11 months and then adjust the final month to stay under the cap.</p>
