<p>You have some extra cash each month after covering essentials. Should you throw it at your credit card balance or funnel it into a brokerage account? This is one of the most common financial dilemmas, and the answer depends on a few key numbers.</p>

<p>The good news is that there is a straightforward framework to make this decision. Once you compare your debt's interest rate against realistic investment returns, the math usually points in a clear direction.</p>

<h2 id="the-interest-rate-decision-rule">The Interest Rate Decision Rule</h2>

<p>Compare your debt's interest rate to the expected return on your investments. The S&P 500 has returned an average of about 10% per year over the past 50 years before inflation. After inflation, that drops to roughly 7%.</p>

<p>If your debt charges more than 7%, paying it off is almost certainly the better move. Credit cards at 20% to 25% APR are a guaranteed drag on your finances that no investment can reliably overcome. Paying off a 22% credit card is the equivalent of earning a guaranteed 22% return.</p>

<p>If your debt is below 5%, such as a subsidized student loan or a low-rate mortgage, investing the difference makes mathematical sense over the long run. The gray zone between 5% and 7% is where personal factors like risk tolerance and peace of mind come into play.</p>

<h2 id="always-capture-free-money-first">Always Capture Free Money First</h2>

<p>If your employer offers a 401(k) match, contribute at least enough to get the full match before sending extra money to debt. A typical match is 50% of your contributions up to 6% of your salary. That is a 50% instant return on your money, which beats paying off even the highest-rate credit card.</p>

<p>According to the <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/112315/how-401k-matching-works.asp" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Investopedia guide on 401(k) matching</a>, leaving employer match money on the table is one of the costliest financial mistakes workers make. Even if you are aggressively paying down debt, this is one investment you should not skip.</p>

<p>After capturing the match, redirect all additional cash toward your highest-rate debt until it is gone. Then gradually increase your investment contributions as each debt is paid off.</p>

<h2 id="the-guaranteed-return-of-debt-payoff">The Guaranteed Return of Debt Payoff</h2>

<p>When you pay off a credit card charging 22% APR, you effectively earn a risk-free 22% return. No stock, bond, or real estate investment offers a guaranteed return anywhere near that. This is why most financial advisors recommend eliminating high-interest debt before investing.</p>

<p>The psychological benefit is real too. Carrying debt adds stress that can cloud your investment decisions. A 2024 survey by the American Psychological Association found that 72% of adults reported feeling stressed about money at least some of the time, with debt being the top cited cause.</p>

<p>Once your high-rate debts are cleared, you free up cash flow that can fund both an <a href="/blog/emergency-fund-building-guide-for-beginners">emergency fund</a> and a solid investment portfolio. The relief of being debt-free also makes it easier to stay consistent with long-term investing.</p>

<h2 id="the-case-for-investing-while-in-debt">The Case for Investing While in Debt</h2>

<p>Time in the market matters. The earlier you start investing, the more years compound interest has to work. A 25-year-old who invests $200 per month for 40 years at 8% average annual returns accumulates about $698,000. Starting at 30 cuts that to roughly $472,000.</p>

<p>Tax-advantaged accounts like Roth IRAs have annual contribution limits that you cannot make up later. If you skip a year, that contribution room is gone forever. For low-rate debt below 5%, investing in a Roth IRA while making minimum debt payments can be the smarter long-term play.</p>

<p>If your employer offers a Roth 401(k) option, you get the benefits of tax-free growth plus the employer match. Review your <a href="/blog/retirement-accounts-explained-ira-vs-401k-vs-roth">retirement account options</a> to find the best fit for your situation.</p>

<h2 id="the-hybrid-approach">The Hybrid Approach</h2>

<p>Many people benefit from splitting extra cash between debt and investing. A common ratio is 70% toward debt and 30% toward investments. This approach lets you make meaningful progress on debt while still building your portfolio.</p>

<p>Start by listing your debts using the <a href="/blog/debt-snowball-vs-debt-avalanche">snowball or avalanche method</a>. Allocate the majority of your extra cash to the target debt, and set up automatic investments for the remainder. As each debt falls off, shift more toward investing.</p>

<p>The hybrid approach also builds the investing habit early. By the time you are debt-free, you already have an established portfolio and the discipline to contribute regularly. Check our <a href="/blog/how-to-start-investing-with-just-100-dollars">beginner investing guide</a> to set up your first investment account alongside your debt payoff plan.</p>

<h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Should I cash out investments to pay off debt?</h3>

<p>Generally no. Selling investments triggers capital gains taxes and potentially early withdrawal penalties in retirement accounts. The tax hit can erase much of the benefit. The exception is if your debt interest rate is extremely high (above 25%) and you have taxable investments with minimal gains. Consult a financial advisor before liquidating. Learn more at <a href="https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/investing/should-i-sell-investments-to-pay-off-debt" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">NerdWallet's analysis on this topic</a>.</p>

<h3>What about student loan debt under 5%?</h3>

<p>Low-rate student loans are among the best candidates for the "invest instead" approach, especially if you are pursuing <a href="/blog/student-loan-repayment-strategies-for-2026">income-driven repayment or PSLF</a>. Make your required payments and direct extra cash into a tax-advantaged investment account. The long-term growth potential outweighs the modest interest cost.</p>

<h3>Does this advice change during a market downturn?</h3>

<p>Market downturns actually make investing more attractive because you buy shares at lower prices. However, if a downturn also threatens your income or job security, prioritizing an emergency fund and debt reduction provides more immediate stability. Adjust your split based on your personal risk level rather than market conditions.</p>
