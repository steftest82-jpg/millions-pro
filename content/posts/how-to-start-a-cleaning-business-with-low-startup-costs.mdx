How to Start a Cleaning Business With Low Startup Costs

Most side hustles demand thousands of dollars before you earn a single penny back. The cleaning business flips that script entirely. You can start a cleaning business for as little as $500 to $2,000, making it one of the most accessible ventures available today. The commercial cleaning industry alone is worth between $262 billion and $468 billion globally in 2025, growing at 4.5% to 7% annually. That means demand is rising and there is plenty of room for newcomers. Whether you want a full time income or a cleaning business side hustle that fits around your current schedule, the barrier to entry is remarkably low. You likely already own many of the supplies you need. This guide walks you through every step, from choosing a niche to landing your first paying clients, with exact dollar amounts so you can plan your cleaning business startup costs with total confidence.

<h2>Choose Your Profitable Cleaning Niche</h2>

Your niche determines everything. It shapes your cleaning business startup costs, your client type, and your earning ceiling. Most beginners default to general residential cleaning, but thinking strategically here saves you money and positions you for faster growth.

Residential cleaning is the cheapest entry point. You need basic supplies, no specialized equipment, and you can start serving clients within days. Commercial cleaning pays more per contract but often requires industrial equipment, insurance upgrades, and after hours availability. Specialty niches like Airbnb turnover cleaning, move out cleaning, and post construction cleanup sit somewhere in between.

Here is a simple comparison to guide your decision. Residential cleaning costs roughly $300 to $800 to start, earns $25 to $50 per hour, and has moderate competition. Commercial cleaning requires $1,500 to $5,000 upfront, earns $30 to $75 per hour, and faces high competition from established firms. Airbnb turnover cleaning costs $400 to $1,000 to launch, earns $75 to $200 per turnover, and has lower competition in most markets.

If you want to start a cleaning business with the lowest possible investment, residential or Airbnb turnover cleaning gives you the best return on your initial spending. Just as choosing the right niche matters when you <a href="/blog/how-to-turn-your-hobby-into-a-profitable-business">turn your hobby into a profitable business</a>, picking the right cleaning niche sets your financial trajectory from day one.

<h2>Start a Cleaning Business Legally</h2>

Getting your legal foundation right protects you and builds client trust. The good news is that legal costs for a low cost cleaning business remain minimal compared to most industries.

First, choose your business structure. A sole proprietorship costs nothing to form in most states and works fine when you are just starting. An LLC offers liability protection and typically costs $50 to $500 depending on your state. The <a href="https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">U.S. Small Business Administration provides a detailed breakdown of each structure</a> to help you decide.

Next, register your business name with your county or state. This usually runs $10 to $50. Apply for a free Employer Identification Number through the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">IRS online EIN application portal</a>. You will need this for taxes even as a sole proprietor.

Check your city and county websites for local business permits or licenses. These range from $25 to $100 in most areas. Some states require no special license for residential cleaning at all.

Finally, get general liability insurance. Expect to pay $30 to $60 per month for a basic policy covering property damage and accidents. This single expense gives clients confidence and protects your personal assets. Your total legal setup should cost between $100 and $700.

<h2>Budget Friendly Supplies and Equipment Essentials</h2>

You do not need a van full of professional equipment to start a cleaning business. A carefully chosen starter kit gets you through your first dozens of jobs without overspending.

Your must have supplies include an all purpose cleaner ($4), glass cleaner ($3), disinfectant spray ($5), a mop and bucket ($15), a vacuum cleaner ($60 to $150), microfiber cloths in a 12 pack ($10), a scrub brush set ($8), rubber gloves ($5), a caddy or tote to carry supplies ($10), and trash bags ($8). That puts your essential kit between $128 and $218.

Nice to have upgrades include a steam cleaner, an extendable duster, knee pads, and a small carpet spot cleaner. Save these purchases for after your first few paying jobs.

Buy supplies at wholesale clubs like Costco or Sam's Club for the best per unit pricing. Dollar stores carry surprisingly effective cleaning basics. Buying in bulk online through Amazon or restaurant supply websites saves 20% to 40% over retail. You can also make effective DIY cleaning solutions using white vinegar, baking soda, and castile soap, cutting your ongoing supply costs by half.

Your total equipment and supply budget for a residential start a cleaning business launch should land between $150 and $500. <h2>Set Pricing That Wins Clients</h2>

Pricing too low burns you out. Pricing too high scares away new clients. The sweet spot comes from understanding your actual costs and your market.

Three common pricing models work for cleaning businesses. Hourly pricing ($25 to $45 per hour) suits beginners because it is simple and flexible. Flat rate pricing ($100 to $250 per standard home) appeals to clients who want predictability. Per square foot pricing ($0.05 to $0.15) works best for larger homes and commercial spaces.

Use this formula to calculate your minimum profitable rate. Add your supply cost per job (roughly $5 to $10), your travel cost ($3 to $8 in fuel), and your desired hourly wage. Then multiply by 1.3 to cover self employment taxes, which the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/self-employment-tax-social-security-and-medicare-taxes" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">IRS sets at 15.3% for 2025</a>. If you want to earn $30 per hour and your costs total $15 per job, your minimum charge for a two hour job is $97.50.

Create three service tiers. A basic clean covers kitchens, bathrooms, and floors. A standard clean adds dusting, vacuuming all rooms, and surface wiping. A deep clean includes everything plus appliance interiors, baseboards, and window tracks. Tiered pricing increases your average job value by 30% to 50%. <h2>Get Clients Without Spending Big</h2>

Marketing a low cost cleaning business does not require a big advertising budget. The most effective client acquisition strategies for new cleaners are free or nearly free.

Start with a Google Business Profile. It is completely free and puts your cleaning business in front of local searchers immediately. According to industry data from 2025, businesses with complete Google profiles receive 70% more visits than those without. Add photos of your work, request reviews from your first clients, and keep your hours and contact information current.

Post in neighborhood Facebook groups and on Nextdoor. Introduce yourself, share a launch special, and offer a referral bonus of $10 to $20 for every new client someone sends your way. Referral programs consistently outperform paid advertising for local service businesses.

Print simple flyers at home or at a library for pennies per page. Target apartment complexes, community bulletin boards, and local laundromats. Partner with real estate agents and property managers who constantly need move out and move in cleaning services. One good partnership can fill your schedule for months.

Build a free website using Google Sites or Carrd to establish credibility online. Include your services, pricing tiers, and a contact form. Your first 10 clients will likely come from personal outreach and community networking rather than any paid channel. <h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>How much does it really cost to start a cleaning business from scratch?</h3>
Most people can start a cleaning business for $500 to $1,500 total. This covers basic legal registration ($100 to $300), essential supplies and equipment ($150 to $500), general liability insurance (first month at $30 to $60), and minimal marketing materials ($20 to $50). If you already own a vacuum and basic cleaning products, your actual out of pocket cost could drop below $300. The key is starting lean with residential clients and reinvesting your early profits into better equipment.

<h3>Can you start a cleaning business as a side hustle while working full time?</h3>
Absolutely. A cleaning business side hustle fits around almost any work schedule. Many successful cleaners start by booking evening or weekend appointments only. You control which jobs you accept and when you work. Start with two to three clients per week, earn $200 to $600 monthly on the side, and scale up as your client base grows. The flexibility makes cleaning one of the most practical side hustles available in 2026 for people who want to build income without quitting their day job.

<h3>Do you need any certifications or training to start a cleaning business?</h3>
No formal certifications are required to start a residential cleaning business in most U.S. states. However, completing a basic cleaning techniques course (many are free on YouTube) helps you work efficiently and deliver professional results. If you plan to offer specialty services like carpet cleaning or biohazard cleanup, specific certifications from organizations like the IICRC add credibility. General liability insurance matters far more than certifications when it comes to winning client trust and protecting yourself financially.
