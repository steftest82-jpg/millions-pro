<h2 id="asset-allocation">Determine Your Asset Allocation</h2>

<p>Asset allocation is the most important investment decision you will make. It determines roughly 90 percent of your portfolio's long-term performance. The primary decision is how much to allocate to stocks versus bonds.</p>

<p>Stocks offer higher expected returns but with more volatility. Bonds offer lower returns but with more stability. Research published by <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/assetallocation.asp" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Investopedia</a> confirms the effectiveness of this strategy.</p>

<p>A common rule of thumb is to subtract your age from 110 to determine your stock allocation percentage. A 30-year-old would hold 80 percent stocks and 20 percent bonds. A 50-year-old would hold 60 percent stocks and 40 percent bonds.</p>

<p>This is a starting point, not a rigid rule. Your specific allocation should reflect your risk tolerance, investment timeline, and financial goals. We cover this in more detail in our guide to <a href="/blog/index-funds-vs-etfs-which-is-right-for-beginners">index funds versus ETFs</a>.</p>

<p>If your investment horizon is 20 years or more, you can afford to hold a higher percentage in stocks because you have time to recover from market downturns. Our guide on <a href="/blog/understanding-market-corrections-and-how-to-respond-as-a-beginner">understanding market corrections</a> explains how to stay composed when those dips happen. If you need the money within five years, a more conservative allocation with higher bond allocation protects against the risk of a market decline at exactly the wrong time.</p>

<h2 id="choose-investments">Choose Your Investments</h2>

<p>The three-fund portfolio is the most elegant and effective approach for most investors. It consists of a US total stock market index fund, an international total stock market index fund, and a US total bond market index fund. These three funds together give you exposure to virtually every investable asset in the world at minimal cost.</p>

<p>This pairs well with our breakdown of <a href="/blog/how-to-open-your-first-brokerage-account-in-2026">opening your first brokerage account</a>. You can find detailed guidelines and resources at <a href="https://www.sec.gov/investor" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">the SEC</a>.</p>

<p>A typical allocation for a young investor might be 60 percent US stocks, 25 percent international stocks, and 15 percent bonds. If you want to understand why that international slice matters, read our <a href="/blog/how-to-invest-in-international-stocks-beginner-guide">beginner guide to investing in international stocks</a>. Within the stock portion, understanding the difference between <a href="/blog/growth-stocks-vs-value-stocks-which-strategy-fits-you">growth stocks and value stocks</a> can help you fine-tune your strategy.</p>

<p>At Vanguard, this would be VTI, VXUS, and BND. At Fidelity, the equivalents are FSKAX, FTIHX, and FXNAX.</p>

<p>The specific funds vary by brokerage but the concept is identical.</p>

<p>An even simpler approach is a single target date fund that holds the same combination of US stocks, international stocks, and bonds but automatically adjusts the allocation as you age. This is the ultimate set-it-and-forget-it option and is an excellent choice if you want zero ongoing management decisions. If you prefer fully automated portfolio management, explore our guide to the <a href="/blog/best-robo-advisors-for-beginner-investors-in-2026">best robo-advisors for beginner investors</a>.</p>

<h2 id="maintain-rebalance">Maintain and Rebalance</h2>

<p>Over time, different asset classes grow at different rates, causing your actual allocation to drift from your target. If stocks outperform bonds for several years, your portfolio might shift from 80/20 to 90/10. Rebalancing means selling some of the outperforming asset and buying more of the underperforming one to return to your target allocation.</p>

<p>Industry professionals often reference <a href="https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/investing/how-to-build-a-portfolio" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">NerdWallet</a> for up-to-date information on this topic.</p>

<p>Rebalance once or twice per year, either on a fixed schedule or when any asset class drifts more than 5 percentage points from its target. In tax-advantaged retirement accounts, rebalancing is free from tax consequences. In taxable accounts, try to rebalance by directing new contributions to the underweight asset class rather than selling.</p>

<p>Looking for the next step? Read about <a href="/blog/five-common-investing-mistakes-beginners-make">common investing mistakes</a>.</p>

<p>The discipline of rebalancing enforces a counterintuitive but profitable behavior: you systematically buy low and sell high. When stocks drop and bonds rise, rebalancing moves money from bonds to stocks while stocks are cheap. When stocks surge, rebalancing takes profits and moves them to the relative safety of bonds.</p>

<p>This disciplined approach adds measurable value over time.</p>

<h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>How much money do I need to start building an investment portfolio?</h3>

<p>You can start with as little as 1 dollar at most major brokerages since they now offer fractional shares. The important thing is to begin investing consistently rather than waiting until you have a large lump sum, because time in the market matters more than the initial amount.</p>

<h3>What is a three-fund portfolio and why is it recommended for beginners?</h3>

<p>A three-fund portfolio consists of a US total stock market index fund, an international total stock market index fund, and a US total bond market index fund. These three funds together give you exposure to virtually every investable asset in the world at minimal cost, providing excellent diversification with zero complexity.</p>

<h3>How often should I rebalance my investment portfolio?</h3>

<p>Rebalance once or twice per year, either on a fixed schedule or when any asset class drifts more than 5 percentage points from its target allocation. In tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs and 401ks, rebalancing is free from tax consequences.</p>

<h3>What is the difference between a target date fund and a three-fund portfolio?</h3>

<p>A target date fund holds the same combination of US stocks, international stocks, and bonds but automatically adjusts the allocation as you age, making it a true set-it-and-forget-it option. A three-fund portfolio gives you more control over the exact percentages but requires you to rebalance manually.</p>

<h3>How do I determine the right stock-to-bond ratio for my age?</h3>

<p>A common starting point is to subtract your age from 110 to get your stock allocation percentage. For example, a 30-year-old would hold roughly 80 percent stocks and 20 percent bonds, but you should adjust based on your personal risk tolerance and investment timeline.</p>
