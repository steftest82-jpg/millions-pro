<p>Your debt-to-income ratio is one of the first numbers a lender checks when you apply for a mortgage, car loan, or personal loan. It tells them how much of your monthly income already goes toward debt payments, and whether you can handle more.</p>

<p>Unlike your credit score, your DTI is not a fixed number in a database. It changes every time your income shifts or you add or pay off a debt. Understanding and managing this ratio gives you a clear advantage when borrowing.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-calculate-your-dti">How to Calculate Your DTI</h2>

<p>Add up all your minimum monthly debt payments. Include your rent or mortgage, car payment, student loans, credit card minimums, and any other recurring debt obligations. Do not include utilities, groceries, or insurance premiums.</p>

<p>Divide that total by your gross monthly income, which is what you earn before taxes and deductions. Multiply by 100 to get a percentage. For example, $2,000 in monthly debt payments divided by $6,000 in gross income equals a DTI of 33%.</p>

<p>If you want a quick calculation, the <a href="https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/explore/compare/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">CFPB's mortgage tools</a> include a DTI calculator that walks you through each step. Run the numbers before applying for any new loan so you know where you stand.</p>

<h2 id="what-lenders-consider-good">What Lenders Consider a Good DTI</h2>

<p>Most conventional mortgage lenders set 43% as the maximum DTI for loan approval. FHA loans may allow up to 50% in some cases with compensating factors like strong reserves or a high credit score. Below 36% is where you get the best rates and easiest approvals.</p>

<p>For personal loans and auto loans, lenders use similar thresholds but may be more flexible. Credit card issuers rarely disclose a DTI cutoff, but they do consider your overall debt load when setting credit limits and interest rates.</p>

<p>A 2025 Freddie Mac report found that borrowers with DTIs above 45% were three times more likely to become delinquent on their mortgage than those below 36%. Keeping your ratio low protects both your borrowing power and your financial stability.</p>

<h2 id="why-your-dti-matters-beyond-loans">Why Your DTI Matters Beyond Loans</h2>

<p>Even if you are not planning to borrow soon, your DTI is a health check for your finances. A high ratio means most of your income is locked into debt payments, leaving little room for savings, investing, or emergencies.</p>

<p>Landlords often check DTI when reviewing rental applications. A ratio above 40% can disqualify you from competitive apartments, even if your credit score is strong. Some employers in financial services also review DTI during background checks.</p>

<p>Tracking your DTI over time gives you a clear picture of whether you are making progress. As you pay down balances, watch the number drop. Pair this tracking with a <a href="/blog/budgeting-apps-that-actually-work-in-2026">budgeting app</a> to see the relationship between your spending habits and your ratio.</p>

<h2 id="five-ways-to-lower-your-dti">Five Ways to Lower Your DTI</h2>

<p>The fastest approach is to pay off small debts entirely. Eliminating a $150 car payment or a $75 credit card minimum immediately drops your DTI by the percentage those payments represent. Use the <a href="/blog/debt-snowball-vs-debt-avalanche">debt snowball method</a> to target small balances first for quick ratio improvements.</p>

<p>Increase your income. A raise, a side job, or freelance work boosts the denominator in the DTI equation. Even an extra $500 per month in gross income can lower a 40% DTI to 36% if your debt payments stay flat.</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Refinance to lower payments.</strong> A lower interest rate on a car loan or student loan reduces your monthly minimum, which directly improves your ratio.</li>
<li><strong>Avoid taking on new debt.</strong> Every new loan or credit card balance raises your DTI. Pause new borrowing until your ratio is where you want it.</li>
<li><strong>Pay more than minimums.</strong> Extra payments reduce balances faster, which lowers future minimum payment amounts and improves your ratio month over month.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="dti-vs-credit-utilization">DTI vs. Credit Utilization</h2>

<p>These two ratios measure different things. Credit utilization looks at how much of your available credit card limits you are using. DTI looks at how much of your income goes to all debt payments. Both matter, but they serve different purposes.</p>

<p>Your credit utilization directly affects your credit score. Your DTI does not appear on your credit report and does not factor into your FICO score. However, lenders calculate it during the application process using documents like pay stubs and tax returns.</p>

<p>Improving both at the same time is ideal. Paying down credit card balances reduces your utilization and your DTI simultaneously. For a deeper dive into credit factors, check our guide on <a href="/blog/understanding-your-credit-score-and-how-to-improve-it">understanding and improving your credit score</a>.</p>

<h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Does rent count in my DTI?</h3>

<p>Yes. When you apply for a mortgage, your current rent payment is included in the DTI calculation. Once you get a mortgage, the mortgage payment (including taxes and insurance) replaces rent in the calculation. All recurring debt obligations count.</p>

<h3>Can I get a mortgage with a DTI above 43%?</h3>

<p>Some loan programs, particularly FHA and VA loans, allow DTIs up to 50% with compensating factors such as large cash reserves, a strong credit score, or a substantial down payment. However, rates will be higher and approval is not guaranteed. Work on lowering your ratio before applying when possible. Learn more from <a href="https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/mortgages/debt-income-ratio-mortgage" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">NerdWallet's mortgage DTI guide</a>.</p>

<h3>How quickly can I lower my DTI?</h3>

<p>If you can pay off a small debt or secure a raise, your DTI can improve within one month. Refinancing a loan to lower the monthly payment also produces an immediate change. For larger improvements, a structured payoff plan over 6 to 12 months can bring a 45% DTI down below the 36% target.
