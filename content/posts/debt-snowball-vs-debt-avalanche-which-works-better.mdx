<h2 id="how-each-works">How Each Method Works</h2>

<p>Both methods start the same way. List all your debts, make minimum payments on everything, and direct any extra money toward one target debt. The difference is which debt you target first.</p>

<p>The experts at <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/080716/debt-avalanche-vs-debt-snowball-which-best-you.asp" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Investopedia</a> provide additional context on this approach.</p>

<p>The debt snowball method targets the smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. You throw every extra dollar at your smallest debt until it is gone, then roll that payment into the next smallest debt. The momentum builds like a snowball rolling downhill, with each eliminated debt freeing up more money for the next one.</p>

<p>See also our deep dive into <a href="/blog/how-to-create-a-debt-payoff-plan-that-actually-works">creating a debt payoff plan</a>.</p>

<p>The debt avalanche method targets the highest interest rate first, regardless of balance. You focus all extra payments on the debt costing you the most in interest charges. Mathematically, this minimizes the total interest you pay across all debts over the entire payoff period.</p>

<h2 id="math-vs-psychology">The Math vs Psychology Debate</h2>

<p>On paper, the avalanche method always wins. By targeting the highest interest rate first, you minimize the total interest paid and become debt-free slightly sooner. The savings can be significant, sometimes thousands of dollars depending on your debt load and interest rates.</p>

<p>If you want to dive deeper, we also wrote about <a href="/blog/how-to-pay-off-credit-card-debt-in-twelve-months">paying off credit card debt fast</a>. Industry professionals often reference <a href="https://www.nerdwallet.com/article/finance/what-is-a-debt-avalanche" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">NerdWallet</a> for up-to-date information on this topic.</p>

<p>However, behavioral research tells a different story. A study published in the Journal of Consumer Research found that people using the snowball method were more likely to successfully eliminate all their debt. The quick wins from paying off smaller balances first create motivational momentum that keeps people engaged with their payoff plan.</p>

<p>The psychological benefit of crossing a debt completely off your list cannot be overstated. Seeing the number of debts decrease from seven to six to five provides tangible proof that your effort is working. This emotional reward sustains motivation through the long months and years that debt payoff typically requires.</p>

<h2 id="choosing-strategy">Choosing Your Strategy</h2>

<p>Choose the avalanche method if you are mathematically driven, patient, and motivated by saving the maximum amount of money. You need to be comfortable with the possibility that your first target debt might take many months to eliminate if it has a large balance. For additional research, <a href="https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/what-is-a-debt-to-income-ratio-en-1791/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau</a> offers comprehensive data on this topic.</p>

<p>Choose the snowball method if you need quick wins to stay motivated, have several small debts that could be eliminated quickly, or have struggled to stick with debt payoff plans in the past. The psychological wins are real and powerful. If you want to dive deeper, we also wrote about <a href="/blog/how-to-negotiate-lower-interest-rates-on-your-debt">negotiating lower interest rates</a>.</p>

<p>A hybrid approach can work well too. You can also layer in the <a href="/blog/debt-snowflake-method-how-micro-payments-accelerate-debt-payoff">debt snowflake method</a>, which directs small windfalls and spare change toward your debt alongside your primary strategy. If your highest interest debt also happens to have a relatively small balance, you get both the mathematical and psychological benefits.</p>

<p>Or you might start with one or two snowball wins to build momentum, then switch to the avalanche method for the remaining larger debts. If multiple people in your household carry debt, learn <a href="/blog/how-to-create-a-family-debt-payoff-plan-together">how to create a family debt payoff plan together</a> so everyone stays aligned.</p>

<h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently Asked Questions</h2>

<h3>Which debt payoff method saves more money in interest?</h3>

<p>The debt avalanche method always saves more money in total interest because it targets the highest interest rate debt first. Depending on your debt load and interest rates, the savings can amount to hundreds or even thousands of dollars compared to the snowball method.</p>

<h3>Why do people choose the debt snowball if the avalanche saves more money?</h3>

<p>Research from the Journal of Consumer Research shows that people using the snowball method are more likely to successfully eliminate all their debt. The quick psychological wins from paying off smaller balances first create motivational momentum that keeps people engaged through the long payoff process.</p>

<h3>Can I combine the debt snowball and debt avalanche methods?</h3>

<p>Yes, a hybrid approach works well. You can start by paying off one or two small debts using the snowball method to build momentum, then switch to the avalanche method for the remaining larger debts to maximize interest savings going forward.</p>

<h3>What is the first step for either the snowball or avalanche method?</h3>

<p>Both methods start identically: list all your debts, make minimum payments on every debt, and direct any extra money toward one target debt. The only difference is whether you target the smallest balance first (snowball) or the highest interest rate first (avalanche).</p>

<h3>How do I stay motivated during a long debt payoff journey?</h3>

<p>Track your progress visually, celebrate each debt you eliminate, and remind yourself of the total interest saved. If motivation wanes with the avalanche method, consider switching to the snowball approach for a quick win before returning to the mathematically optimal strategy.</p>
